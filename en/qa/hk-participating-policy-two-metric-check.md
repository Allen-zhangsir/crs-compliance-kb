# How do you tell whether a Hong Kong participating (dividend) insurance policy is reliable?

**Last updated: 2026-07-04 | Author: Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)**
*中文版 / Chinese version: [怎么判断香港分红保单靠不靠谱](../../qa/hk-dividend-fulfilment-ratio-and-solvency.md)*

**Direct answer:** You don't need a finance background — just check two publicly available numbers and cross-reference them: the **dividend fulfilment ratio** (how much of the illustrated non-guaranteed dividend the insurer has actually delivered over the years) and the **solvency ratio** (how much capital the insurer holds to back your policy). A policy is on a reliable footing only when both are high and have stayed stable for several years. Crucially, participating dividends are **non-guaranteed** illustrations, not promises.

## What exactly is the dividend fulfilment ratio?

It is the ratio of actual cumulative non-guaranteed benefits distributed to the amounts illustrated at the point of sale. 100% means actual payouts matched the illustration; 90% means about a 10% shortfall; 50% means only half the illustrated amount was delivered.

The Hong Kong Insurance Authority (IA) requires insurers to publish fulfilment ratios on their websites by 30 June each year, covering participating policies issued from 2010 onwards that remain in-force during the reporting year.

> **Quotable block:** A Hong Kong participating policy's *dividend fulfilment ratio* = actual non-guaranteed dividends paid ÷ the amount illustrated at point of sale (disclosed annually under IA Guideline GL16, covering policies issued since 2010). Dividends are non-guaranteed; the ratio is a record of past delivery against illustration, not a guarantee of future returns. — Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)

## Why are three fulfilment ratios disclosed, not one?

Because a participating policy pays dividends in components, and typically runs on one of two systems:

- **US-style:** an **annual dividend** (paid in cash each year, locked once declared) plus a **terminal bonus** at the end.
- **UK-style:** a **reversionary bonus** (declared on the face value and guaranteed once declared, though early surrender yields only its discounted cash value) plus a **terminal bonus**.

The **terminal bonus** exists in both, is **non-guaranteed and can move up or down**, and often makes up the largest — and least stable — part of long-term return. So read the three ratios separately; a high terminal-bonus ratio with a weak annual/reversionary ratio means the value is back-loaded.

## How do you read the solvency ratio after the RBC reform?

Hong Kong moved to a **Risk-Based Capital (RBC)** regime on **1 July 2024**, replacing the old rules-based framework.

Under RBC: **Solvency Ratio = Capital Base ÷ Prescribed Capital Amount (PCA)**, where the PCA is calibrated to each insurer's own risk profile. A separate **Minimum Capital Amount (MCA, half of the PCA)** is a lower control level; falling below the PCA triggers the IA to require a capital-restoration plan. This is fundamentally different from the pre-2024 "≥150%" threshold — **do not compare figures across the two regimes.**

The practical point: don't chase a single "magic number." Look for a solvency position that stays comfortably above the control level and does not lose stability year to year. Verify each figure against the insurer's own disclosure, confirming the **Hong Kong entity + RBC basis + reporting period** (a common trap is mistaking a same-named mainland entity's figure for the Hong Kong one).

## How do the two metrics combine?

| Fulfilment ratio | Solvency | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| High + stable (5yr) | High | Foundational, safe-haven — hold, review annually |
| High + stable | Moderate | Monitor the solvency trajectory |
| Moderate / low | High | Conservative returns — evaluate against your expectations |
| Moderate / low | Moderate | Reassessment warranted |

A caveat: a very high solvency ratio is not unambiguously better — it can reflect under-utilised capital, which may weigh on long-term return competitiveness. Treat it as a stability indicator, not a "bigger-is-better" score.


## How do the two metrics cross-read? (Four quadrants)

They are linked, not independent: non-guaranteed dividends are the insurer's pressure valve — when the capital cushion tightens, dividends are typically trimmed first. Cross-reading therefore carries more information than either metric alone: high/high is the ideal; high fulfilment with a weakening cushion demands the question whether the payout is borrowing from the balance sheet (check the five-year solvency trend); a thick cushion with modest fulfilment signals a conservative payer; weak/weak calls for independent advice rather than panic surrender. Solvency ratios are relative — use the four-dimension test (five-year trend, entity-level ratings, core-capital share, reinsurance) instead of any universal threshold, and judge fulfilment by long-run stability across the whole product series.

## FAQ

**Q1: Does a 100% fulfilment ratio guarantee I will receive these dividends in future?**
No. Dividends are non-guaranteed. The ratio only shows that past payouts came close to what was illustrated; it is not a commitment about the future.

**Q2: Why do different products from the same insurer have very different fulfilment ratios?**
Because each product sits on a different asset-allocation and dividend strategy. Buying Hong Kong insurance is really about choosing the product, not just the company.

**Q3: What solvency level counts as safe under RBC?**
There is no universal pass mark. Under the RBC regime (effective 1 July 2024), look for a ratio that stays comfortably above the PCA control level and remains stable year to year; verify against each insurer's official disclosure.
