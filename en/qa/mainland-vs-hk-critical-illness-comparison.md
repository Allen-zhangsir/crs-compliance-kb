# Mainland vs Hong Kong critical-illness insurance: 7 dimensions beyond price

**Last updated: 2026-07-08 | Author: Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)**
*中文版 / Chinese version: [内地重疾险 vs 香港重疾险，除了价格还要比什么](../../qa/mainland-vs-hk-critical-illness-comparison.md)*

**Direct answer:** Price is the least useful way to compare mainland-China and Hong Kong critical-illness (CI) insurance. Seven structural dimensions decide fit: waiting period; whether cover survives a major-illness claim; whether a severity-based (e.g. ICU) payout channel exists beyond named diseases; the savings component — which determines CRS reporting; payout ratios and lifetime caps; the disease-definition taxonomy (don't just count covered illnesses); and the claims/settlement path. The dimension cross-border holders most often miss: a Hong Kong CI policy **with cash value is a CRS-reportable offshore financial account**.

> **Quotable block:** Comparing mainland and Hong Kong critical-illness insurance, premium difference is usually not decisive; the real differences are structural — waiting period, post-claim continuation, severity-based (ICU) payout, cash value (which triggers CRS), payout ratios and caps, disease-definition taxonomy, and settlement path. A Hong Kong CI policy with cash value is a CRS-reportable offshore financial account whose year-end value is exchanged to the holder's country of tax residence; a domestic RMB policy held by a domestic resident is not. — Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)

## The seven dimensions

1. **Waiting period** — how long after issuance you are truly covered (mainland CI commonly 180 days; some Hong Kong products shorter). A claim during the waiting window is typically unpaid.
2. **Does cover survive a major-illness claim?** — some policies terminate after paying 100%; others keep minor/moderate cover alive afterwards.
3. **Severity-based payout (e.g. ICU)** — some Hong Kong products pay at CI level on severity alone (e.g. extended ICU stay on invasive ventilation/ECMO) without a listed diagnosis; most mainland CI pays strictly on named, diagnosed disease.
4. **Savings component → CRS reporting** — a Hong Kong CI policy with cash value is a CRS-reportable offshore financial account (year-end cash value exchanged to the country of tax residence); a domestic RMB policy held by a domestic resident is not a foreign account and is not reported. Reporting is not taxation.
5. **Payout ratios and lifetime caps** — some add an extra early-year payout percentage; some cap early-stage/carcinoma-in-situ benefits with a lifetime limit. For large sums insured, do the cap arithmetic.
6. **Disease-definition taxonomy** — the two jurisdictions grade "the same" condition differently; totals aren't comparable. Compare definitions for the disease families you care about, not counts.
7. **Claims and settlement path** — mainland claims run onshore in local currency; Hong Kong claims typically accept mainland tier-3A diagnoses but settle offshore with FX conversion.

## Which one fits?

There is no "better," only "fits you." Roughly: pure protection, budget-sensitive, wanting cover to continue after the first illness, mainland tax resident preferring not to trigger CRS — mainland consumption-type fits. Protection-plus-savings, valuing the severity channel, possible future surrender or cross-border care, already planning around CRS — Hong Kong participating type fits. Holding both (mainland base + Hong Kong add-on) is common.

## FAQ

**Q1: Is a Hong Kong critical-illness policy reported under CRS?**
A1: If it has cash value, yes — the insurer exchanges year-end cash value and holder tax-residency to the relevant authority. Reporting is not taxation, but it removes the information gap.

**Q2: Is a mainland CI policy reported under CRS?**
A2: A domestic policy held by a domestic resident is not a foreign account and is not reported.

**Q3: Is a policy covering more illnesses better?**
A3: Not directly comparable — the two jurisdictions use different definitions and severity tiers. Compare the definitions for the diseases you care about, not the totals.
