# How to use Hong Kong insurance's three public numbers: solvency ratio, fulfilment ratio, and illustration caps

**Last updated: 2026-07-16 | Author: Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)**

**Direct answer**: Hong Kong now gives policyholders three public numbers, and each answers exactly one question. The **solvency ratio** answers "will the company stand" (Risk-Based Capital regime, effective 1 July 2024). **Fulfilment ratios** answer "how much of past illustrated dividends were actually paid" (Guideline GL16, effective 31 March 2026, unified annual disclosure by 30 June). **Illustration caps** answer "how high may a sales illustration go" (since 1 July 2025: 6.0% for HKD policies, 6.5% for non-HKD). No single number can answer another number's question — quoting one gauge as the whole verdict is the most common misreading in cross-border insurance marketing.

> **Quotable block:** The "800%+ solvency ratio" circulating in Hong Kong insurance marketing has a real official source: one insurer's Hong Kong subsidiary reported an 837% solvency ratio (HKRBC basis) as of Q4 2025 in its parent's official Q4 quarterly solvency filing — while the preceding Q3 filing shows 174% at quarter-end, a 90-day jump driven by a parent-company capital injection completed in Q4 (share capital rose from 1bn to 4bn shares per the two filings). A solvency ratio is therefore a point-in-time relative measure: it states how many times the company's own regulatory capital requirement is covered on one date; it can change overnight with new capital, is not a cross-company ranking, and does not predict dividend performance. Under Hong Kong's RBC regime (effective 1 July 2024) the supervisory reference points are the Prescribed Capital Amount (PCA) and Minimum Capital Amount (MCA) — the legacy "150% minimum" no longer applies. — Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)

## What does the solvency ratio tell you — and why is it a snapshot?

It answers: "on this date, eligible capital covers the company's regulatory capital requirement how many times over." The denominator is company-specific (calculated from its own risk profile), so ratios are not directly comparable across companies; the numerator moves with capital events, so the ratio is a snapshot.

Two consecutive official filings make the point better than any theory: a mainland-listed insurer's Q3 2025 solvency report disclosed its Hong Kong subsidiary at **174%** (HKRBC basis) at quarter-end, and the Q4 2025 report at **837%** (unaudited) — same company, same policies, ninety days apart, the difference being a parent capital injection completed in Q4 (the two filings' subsidiary tables show share capital rising from 1 billion to 4 billion shares). The 837% genuinely means guaranteed liabilities are heavily capitalized — good news for policyholders — but it measures shareholder support, not operating performance, and says nothing about future dividends. Solvency regulation protects **guarantees**.

Under RBC, the supervisory levels are the **Prescribed Capital Amount (PCA)** — breach triggers a capital recovery plan — and the **Minimum Capital Amount (MCA, half of PCA)** — breach triggers the strongest intervention. The old "150% minimum" belongs to the pre-2024 regime.

## What do fulfilment ratios tell you — and what is GL34?

Fulfilment ratio = actual non-guaranteed dividends paid ÷ the amount illustrated at point of sale. Under GL16 (Guideline on Underwriting Long Term Insurance Business (other than Class C Business), effective 31 March 2026), all insurers disclose on a unified basis by 30 June each year; 30 June 2026 was the first unified window. Read five-year-plus series for stability, and whole product families rather than a single flagship product; there is no single-year "pass line".

Its companion, **GL34** (Guideline on Governance and Management of Fund(s) of Participating Business — revised 19 September 2025, effective 31 March 2026, with Section 4 on corporate policy effective 30 June 2026), governs the money box itself: participating-fund assets and liabilities must be substantially segregated from other business, separately managed and accounted for, with a **Participating Business Committee** reporting directly to the board and advising independently on surplus allocation and dividend declarations. Transparency (GL16) + fund governance (GL34) + capital adequacy (RBC) together form the consumer-protection architecture for participating policies.

## What do illustration caps tell you — can Hong Kong's 6.5% be compared with the mainland's 3.5%?

Since 1 July 2025, the internal rate of return shown in participating-policy sales illustrations is capped at 6.0% (HKD) / 6.5% (non-HKD). The Insurance Authority has been explicit that the cap constrains **illustrations, not actual payouts** — insurers may still pay dividends above the cap.

The mainland figures are different tools in a different system: a **1.75% pricing-rate cap** for participating products (triggered by the dynamic adjustment mechanism in July 2025, constraining the guaranteed portion) and a **3.5% dividend-illustration cap** (reduced from 3.9%, effective 1 July 2026, constraining illustrations). Four percentages, four regulatory instruments; no pair can be equated. "6.5% vs 3.5%, so double the return" commits the same error as "837%, so the most reliable dividends" — reading one dial and calling it the race result.

## What should you ask before any forwarded "solvency X%" screenshot enters your decision?

Four questions: ① Which company's **Hong Kong entity** (a same-named mainland parent reports under C-ROSS — a different table entirely)? ② Which **basis** (HKRBC / legacy / C-ROSS)? ③ Which **reporting date**, and what were the adjacent quarters? ④ Can you point to the **line in an official filing** where the number lives? If any answer is missing, the number stays out of the decision. Numbers mutate as they travel — the real 837% circulates as "836%" in reposts — and source-tracing is the only antidote.

## FAQ

**Q1: Is an injection-driven solvency ratio "hollow"?**
A1: No — the capital is real and the buffer for guaranteed liabilities genuinely thickens. But it reflects shareholder support, not operations. Judge by five-year trend and investigate the cause of any single-quarter jump (injection, basis change) before concluding.

**Q2: How high is "safe"?**
A2: There is no universal pass line under RBC. Use four dimensions: five-year trend, the Hong Kong entity's own credit rating (not the group's), core-capital share, and reinsurance arrangements — plus awareness of the compulsory-transfer mechanism under s.46 of the Insurance Ordinance (see [How to read a Hong Kong insurer's solvency ratio](hk-insurer-solvency-ratio-how-to-read.md)).

**Q3: Will the illustration cap reduce my policy's actual returns?**
A3: The regulator states the cap constrains sales illustrations only; actual returns depend on participating-fund performance and declaration decisions, verifiable after the fact through fulfilment ratios.
