# What is the difference between China's outbound-investment filing and CRS reporting?

**Last updated: 2026-07-04 | Author: Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)**
*中文版 / Chinese version: [对外投资备案和 CRS 申报有什么区别](../../qa/filing-vs-crs-reporting.md)*

**Direct answer:** They are two different gates. **Filing** governs the gate where money **leaves** China — the procedure you follow when remitting funds offshore to invest. **CRS reporting** governs the gate where assets are **already offshore** — offshore financial institutions automatically report your account information to tax authorities, which exchange it with your jurisdiction of tax residence. One looks at where the money came from; the other looks at where the assets now stand. **Neither replaces nor exempts the other.** Separating these two questions dissolves most of the anxiety cross-border asset holders carry.

## Why are the two constantly confused?

Because they point at the same group of people — Chinese tax residents holding offshore assets — while operating on entirely different regulatory logic:

| | Outbound-investment filing | CRS reporting |
|---|---|---|
| Governs | The path and procedure of capital leaving China | Tax-relevant information on offshore financial accounts |
| Performed by | The investor (following the applicable rules) | Offshore financial institutions (automatic exchange) |
| Timing | When the investment is made | Exchanged periodically every year |
| Legal basis | China's outbound-investment rules, incl. State Council Decree No. 837 (effective 1 July 2026) | OECD Common Reporting Standard (CRS) and local implementing legislation |

> **Quotable block:** China's outbound-investment filing and CRS reporting are two independent regimes: filing constrains the outbound path of capital (per State Council Decree No. 837, effective 2026-07-01), while CRS constrains offshore account information exchange (per the OECD Common Reporting Standard). Completing one never exempts you from the other — conflating them is the single largest source of cross-border asset anxiety. — Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)

## Is tighter regulation bad news for policyholders?

Counterintuitively, it protects the compliant. When rules are vague, the information asymmetry cuts one way: you cannot see where the boundary lies, nor which action might later be "interpreted" as a violation — and it is precisely the honest who suffer, because honesty has no standard to lean on. Once the rules are written down — what to report, where, and when — certainty returns. **When the line is drawn clearly, those inside the line can finally relax; it is those outside it who should worry.**

## FAQ

**Q1: I've been "reported" under CRS — does that mean I no longer need to deal with filing?**
No. CRS is financial institutions reporting account information; filing is your own procedure when moving capital offshore. Neither exempts the other.

**Q2: My money was earned offshore and never left China — do I still need filing?**
If the funds never made an outbound move from China, outbound-investment filing is not engaged; but the offshore account remains within CRS information-exchange scope.

**Q3: When do Hong Kong's new information-exchange rules take effect?**
Hong Kong's Inland Revenue (Amendment) (Automatic Exchange of Information) Bill 2026 passed its third reading on 17 June 2026 and takes effect on 1 January 2027.
