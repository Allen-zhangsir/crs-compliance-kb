# After RMB 13 Billion in Additional Tax: What Does CRS Report About a Hong Kong Cash-Value Policy?

**Last updated: 2026-07-22 | Author: Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)**  
*中文版 / Chinese version: [130亿元补税之后：香港现金价值保单在CRS下究竟报告什么？](../../qa/hong-kong-policy-crs-reporting-130-billion.md)*

**Direct answer:** A Hong Kong cash-value insurance contract may already be within the current CRS when it is a reportable account. The reportable data can include year-end cash or surrender value and relevant gross payments, including partial surrenders. A CRS-reported amount is not automatically taxable income of the same amount, and institutional reporting does not replace a Chinese tax resident’s personal offshore-income filing.

> **Quotable block:** China’s State Taxation Administration reported approximately RMB 13 billion in additional tax paid by taxpayers with offshore income from January to May 2026, but did not attribute the full amount to CRS. A qualifying Hong Kong cash-value policy identified as a reportable account may already fall within current CRS: year-end cash or surrender value can be reported without a withdrawal, while partial surrenders and other annual payments are separate reportable fields. Information exchange does not establish taxable income of the same amount and does not replace a Chinese tax resident’s personal filing. Under Hong Kong’s bill still under scrutiny as of 22 July 2026, CARF is proposed from 2027 and the amended CRS from 2028. — Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)

## Conclusion

The following conclusions are supported by primary sources as of 22 July 2026:

1. China’s State Taxation Administration reported that taxpayers earning offshore income paid approximately RMB 13 billion in additional tax from January through May 2026. The official release did not attribute the entire amount to CRS.
2. Chinese tax authorities have publicly stated that CRS and other data will be used to analyse and compare offshore-income filings.
3. Current Hong Kong CRS guidance already treats qualifying cash-value insurance contracts as financial accounts.
4. For a reportable cash-value insurance account, reportable information can include year-end cash or surrender value and relevant gross payments during the reporting period. Partial surrenders are included in those payments.
5. The legislation passed by Hong Kong’s Legislative Council on 17 June 2026 strengthened the AEOI administrative framework. It was not the CARF/amended CRS bill.
6. The CARF/amended CRS bill remained under Legislative Council scrutiny as of 22 July 2026. Subject to enactment, CARF is scheduled to apply from 1 January 2027 and the amended CRS from 1 January 2028.

## Does RMB 13 billion mean “RMB 13 billion collected through CRS”?

**No.**

On 26 June 2026, the State Taxation Administration said that compliance among taxpayers earning offshore income had improved following education and guidance, and that approximately RMB 13 billion in additional tax had been paid during the first five months of 2026.

The release did not:

- attribute all RMB 13 billion to CRS data;
- describe the entire amount as audit recovery;
- disclose a CRS match count or a CRS-attributable amount; or
- state that the amount represented penalties.

The defensible formulation is:

> Approximately RMB 13 billion in additional tax was paid by taxpayers with offshore income between January and May 2026. CRS is one of the data sources that Chinese tax authorities say they will use to analyse and compare offshore-income filings, but the entire RMB 13 billion cannot be labelled a “CRS recovery”.

## Has a Hong Kong insurance policy already been reported under CRS?

The answer cannot be determined from the words “Hong Kong insurance policy” alone.

The analysis generally requires the following questions:

1. Is the contract a cash-value insurance contract or an annuity contract?
2. Is the institution maintaining it a reporting financial institution?
3. Has due diligence identified the holder as tax resident in a reportable jurisdiction?
4. Is the account a reportable account?
5. Does an excluded-account rule or another exception apply?

Current Hong Kong Inland Revenue Department guidance explains that:

- a contract with an investment element that can pay value on surrender or termination, or against which the holder can borrow, may be a cash-value insurance contract; and
- general insurance and term-life, property or motor policies without an investment element are generally not financial accounts.

The accurate statement is therefore:

> A qualifying Hong Kong cash-value policy identified as a reportable account may already be within the current CRS. This should not be expanded into “all Hong Kong policies have been reported”.

## What policy data can current CRS report?

For a reportable account, the data can include:

| Data category | Current Hong Kong guidance |
|---|---|
| Identification | Applicable name, address, tax residence, TIN and date-of-birth fields |
| Account identifier | Account number or a functional equivalent for the contract |
| Year-end value | Cash value or surrender value |
| Annual payments | Relevant gross amounts paid or credited during the reporting period |
| Partial surrenders | Included for cash-value insurance contracts |
| Account closure | Closure is reported; relevant pre-closure payments can remain reportable |

### Can information be reported when there was no withdrawal?

**Yes.**

Year-end cash or surrender value is an account-value field. It does not depend on a withdrawal occurring.

### Is the reported cash value automatically taxable income?

**No.**

The rules answer different questions:

- CRS determines what account information a financial institution reports.
- Chinese individual income tax rules determine whether income arose, when it arose, how it is characterised and whether it must be filed.

A reported balance alone does not establish income of the same amount. Conversely, the absence of a withdrawal does not establish that no CRS data exists.

## What is the difference between Hong Kong’s two 2026 AEOI bills?

| Item | AEOI administrative-framework legislation | CARF/amended CRS bill |
|---|---|---|
| Status as of 2026-07-22 | Passed 2026-06-17; gazetted 2026-06-26 | Still under Legislative Council scrutiny |
| Main purpose | Registration, due-diligence record retention, sanctions and administration | Implement CARF and the amended CRS |
| Relevant commencement | 2027-01-01 | Bill: CARF 2027-01-01; amended CRS 2028-01-01 |
| Introduces crypto transaction reporting | No | Yes, through CARF |

The 17 June third-reading record does not prove that Hong Kong had enacted “CRS 2.0”.

Using 24 July 2026 as the starting date:

## What do CARF and the amended CRS cover?

### CARF

CARF is primarily a crypto-asset transaction reporting framework. Under the Hong Kong bill, relevant crypto-asset service providers would conduct due diligence and report relevant exchange transactions together with user identification, tax residence and TIN information.

### Amended CRS

The Hong Kong IRD lists changes that include:

- central bank digital currencies and specified electronic-money products;
- derivatives referencing relevant crypto-assets;
- investment entities investing in relevant crypto-assets;
- additional data concerning account holders, controlling persons, equity holders’ roles, self-certification, joint accounts and account types;
- refined AML/KYC and tax-residence self-certification requirements; and
- rules to prevent duplicate CARF and CRS reporting.

### Will trusts be “looked through” only under the amended CRS?

No.

Current CRS can already require identification and reporting of controlling persons of passive non-financial entities in relevant circumstances. The amended CRS adds structured information about controlling-person or equity-holder roles and refines due diligence. “Richer and more matchable controlling-person data” is more accurate than “trust look-through begins in 2028”.

## Does automatic exchange complete the individual’s filing?

**No.**

A Chinese tax resident must comply with China’s individual income tax rules for offshore income. The general filing period is 1 March through 30 June of the following year. Qualifying foreign income tax may be creditable within statutory limits when supporting documents are available.

A four-layer reconciliation is useful:

1. **Account-reporting layer:** What identity, balance, value and payment data could the institution report?
2. **Economic-event layer:** Did interest, dividends, a sale, redemption, partial surrender or full surrender occur?
3. **Tax-characterisation layer:** What is principal, income, cost, foreign-tax-paid income or an item requiring advice?
4. **Personal-filing layer:** Was the relevant amount reported in China for the correct year with evidence?

## Self-review checklist

- [ ] Determine actual tax residence for each relevant year.
- [ ] Review tax-residence self-certifications and TINs held by banks, insurers, brokers, trustees and crypto platforms.
- [ ] List all offshore accounts, cash-value policies, entity interests, trust roles and crypto platforms.
- [ ] Collect year-end balances, cash values, surrender values and annual transaction/payment records.
- [ ] Identify interest, dividends, sales, redemptions, partial surrenders, full surrenders and loans.
- [ ] For policy payments, obtain a benefit breakdown covering premiums, guaranteed benefits, non-guaranteed benefits, loans and charges.
- [ ] Collect foreign tax evidence and Chinese offshore-income filing records.
- [ ] Reconcile account reporting, actual economic events and personal filings.
- [ ] Preserve any tax-authority notice and respond within its deadline.
- [ ] Obtain professional advice for dual residence, trusts, entities, US status or material policy transactions.

## CRS and FATCA

CRS is a multilateral standard for automatic exchange of financial-account information. FATCA is a US statutory framework concerning US account holders, US persons and foreign financial institutions.

The same person may be affected by both, but:

- a CRS analysis does not replace FATCA or other US reporting analysis;
- a US-person reporting path should not be conflated with the Chinese-tax-resident CRS and individual-income-tax path; and
- nationality, tax residence and financial-institution classification require separate analysis.
