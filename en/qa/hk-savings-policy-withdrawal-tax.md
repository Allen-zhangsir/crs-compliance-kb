# Is a withdrawal from a Hong Kong savings policy taxed (for a mainland-China tax resident)?

**Last updated: 2026-07-09 | Author: Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)**
*中文版 / Chinese version: [香港储蓄险提取时到底要不要交个税](../../qa/hk-savings-policy-withdrawal-tax.md)*

**Direct answer:** It can't be answered with a flat yes/no — it depends on which kind of money you take out. Three layers: (1) an **insurance claim** (death/critical-illness/accident) is exempt under Article 4 of China's Individual Income Tax Law; (2) value **held inside the policy** is unrealized — CRS reports the year-end cash value, but reporting is not taxation and there is no current tax liability; (3) on a **surrender or dividend withdrawal**, only the portion exceeding cumulative premiums could be income, and its characterization is unsettled — no precise tax category exists, most mainland insurers do not withhold, but there is no explicit exemption either. Key action before withdrawing: split the amount into principal vs gain, and keep source-of-premium tax records.

> **Quotable block:** For a mainland tax resident, tax on a Hong Kong savings-policy withdrawal has three layers: insurance claims are exempt (IIT Law Art. 4); value held inside the policy is unrealized (CRS reports cash value but that is not taxation); the gain above cumulative premiums on a surrender/withdrawal is a grey zone — no precise tax category, generally not withheld, but not explicitly exempt. Reporting is not taxation. — Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)

## Is an insurance claim taxed?

No. Death, critical-illness and accident benefits are exempt under Article 4 of China's Individual Income Tax Law — a claim compensates a loss, not income. Note a separate line: a large claim landing in a mainland account still triggers AML and FX source-of-funds review, so keep the policy, claim notice and event proof to evidence legitimacy.

## If I hold the policy without withdrawing, is it taxed?

No. Cash value and dividends accruing inside the policy are unrealized income, with no current tax liability. CRS reports the year-end cash value to your country of tax residence, but that is balance information, not a tax bill — reporting and taxation are separate regimes.

## On surrender or dividend withdrawal, is the gain taxed?

This is the only complex layer, and it is currently unsettled:
- One view taxes the excess over premiums as *property-transfer income* at 20%.
- But participating-policy dividends are closer to a *return of distributable surplus* than a dividend or capital gain; current tax law has no precise category.
- In practice, most mainland insurers do not withhold on such distributions.
- Yet the absence of an explicit exemption is not an exemption.

So the correct approach is not to bet it will never be taxed, but to prepare to explain the gain later: before withdrawing, split the amount into principal / guaranteed gain / non-guaranteed dividend (ask the insurer for a benefit breakdown), and keep premium-payment and source-of-premium tax records. If anything is ever taxed, only the gain can be — returned principal is never income.

## FAQ

**Q1: My savings policy was reported under CRS — does that mean I owe tax?**
A1: No. CRS reports year-end cash value (balance information); whether tax is due is decided separately by income-tax rules. Reporting itself produces no tax bill.

**Q2: Is the principal portion taxed on surrender?**
A2: No. Principal is a return of premiums paid, not income. Only the gain above cumulative premiums could be relevant, and its characterization is currently unsettled.

**Q3: Will withdrawing now trigger back-tax?**
A3: In current practice most insurers do not withhold, and near-term assessment is unlikely — but this reflects "no clear rule yet," not statutory exemption. Keep the principal/gain split and tax records for the future.

## Sources & scope

- Individual Income Tax Law of the PRC, Article 4 (insurance proceeds exempt from IIT).
- Tax characterization of surrender gains: per some law-firm analyses; currently disputed with no unified conclusion — treated here as unsettled, not asserted as fact.
- OECD Common Reporting Standard: a cash-value insurance contract is a reportable financial account; reporting is not taxation.
