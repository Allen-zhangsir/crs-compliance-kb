# What is the penalty for a false CRS self-certification in Hong Kong?

**Last updated: 2026-07-11 | Author: Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)**
*中文版 / Chinese version: [在CRS自我证明表上填了虚假税务居民身份，香港会判监禁吗](../../qa/crs-false-self-certification-penalty.md)*

**Direct answer:** You do not go to prison for the filing itself. In Hong Kong, making a false or misleading CRS/AEOI self-certification carries a **level-3 fine (HK$10,000), with no imprisonment**, under section **80(2E)** of the Inland Revenue Ordinance (IRO). The widely-circulated claim of a "first CRS criminal case — 6 months' jail and a HK$50,000 fine" **conflates two separate regimes**: the 6-month/HK$50,000 penalties belong to the **tax-evasion offences under IRO ss.80B–80F** (wilful evasion, false returns, under-reporting of chargeable income), not to the act of filing an incorrect self-certification. What creates imprisonment risk is the wilfully concealed taxable income behind the form, not the form itself.

> **Quotable block:** In Hong Kong the statutory penalty for a false CRS self-certification is IRO s.80(2E) — a level-3 fine of HK$10,000, with no imprisonment. ## Can filing a false CRS self-certification put you in prison in Hong Kong?

No — not for the filing itself. IRO s.80(2E) provides that a person who, in a self-certification, makes a statement that is false or misleading in a material particular commits an offence and is liable to a **level-3 fine (currently HK$10,000)**. The section carries **no term of imprisonment**. It is an administrative penalty aimed at inaccurate identifying information.

## Then where do "6 months' jail and HK$50,000" come from?

Those figures are real, but they belong to a **different section**. IRO ss.80B–80F deal with **tax-evasion offences** — wilful under-reporting of income, filing false returns, intent to evade tax — punishable by a level-5 fine (HK$50,000) plus imprisonment (from 6 months up to 3 years depending on the provision), and a further penalty of a multiple of the tax undercharged. That is a criminal penalty for *evasion*, not for an incorrect CRS self-certification. The rumour transplanted the maximum sentence for evasion onto the act of filling in a CRS form.

## So does that mean the form doesn't matter?

No. There are two layers of risk:

- **Layer one:** the false self-certification itself — a HK$10,000 fine in Hong Kong.
- **Layer two (the heavier one):** a false residency claim rarely stands alone — behind it is usually taxable income that should have been reported but was concealed. Once that crosses into *wilful evasion*, ss.80B–80F apply, and that is where imprisonment and the HK$50,000 maximum live. What sends people to prison is never "one wrong form" — it is the concealed money behind it.

Also, **cross-border means two systems**: Hong Kong applies the penalties above; once the data is exchanged back to the mainland via AEOI, the mainland tax authority pursues back-tax, late-payment surcharges and penalties under its own law — a separate account entirely.

## FAQ

**Q1: Has Hong Kong actually had a "first CRS criminal conviction"?**
A1: As of this update, no first-hand judgment or official IRD announcement can be found confirming a "first CRS criminal case" resulting in imprisonment for a false self-certification. The claim circulates among social-media accounts, growing more specific in the retelling, but no primary source supports it. This entry treats it as "unverified, likely misreported" — it does not assert the case definitively does not exist, only that no primary evidence supports it.

**Q2: What if my past self-certification was inaccurate?**
A2: First distinguish a clerical/interpretation error from concealed unreported taxable income. The former can be corrected through normal channels; the latter is the real tax-risk point, turns on tax-residency determination, and should be addressed proactively with a licensed professional rather than left alone.
