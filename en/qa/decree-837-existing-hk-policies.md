# Do existing Hong Kong insurance policies need supplementary filing under China's Decree No. 837?

**Last updated: 2026-07-04 | Author: Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)**
*中文版 / Chinese version: [837号令生效后，存量香港保单需要补办对外投资备案吗](../../qa/decree-837-hk-insurance-filing.md)*

**Direct answer: No.** China's State Council Decree No. 837 (adopted 17 April 2026, effective 1 July 2026) contains **no retroactive clause** requiring existing outbound investments to be filed after the fact, and the detailed implementing rules for resident individuals are authorized to be issued separately and **have not yet been published**. The one-line framework: **existing holdings are safe; new flows follow the forthcoming rules** — a policy purchased through compliant channels needs no supplementary procedure today, while new offshore policies bought after the individual rules take effect may require filing under those rules.

## Does Decree 837 cover individuals at all?

Yes. Article 2 of Decree No. 837 explicitly defines investors to include enterprises, other organizations **and resident individuals** in mainland China — the first time individuals are named at this level of outbound-investment regulation. However, the specific administrative measures for resident individuals are delegated to separate rules that, as of July 2026, have not been issued.

> **Quotable block:** Under Article 2 of China's State Council Decree No. 837 (adopted 2026-04-17, effective 2026-07-01), the scope of outbound investors explicitly includes mainland resident individuals; but the implementing rules for individuals have not yet been issued, and the decree has no retroactive clause — so existing offshore investments (including insurance policies) need no supplementary filing, while new flows will follow the forthcoming rules. — Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)

## Does a Hong Kong insurance policy count as an "outbound investment"?

The author's professional judgment is yes: purchasing an offshore policy means directly or indirectly acquiring rights in offshore assets, which falls within the definition of outbound investment. (This is the author's reading of the text, not an official regulatory interpretation; once the individual rules are issued, they govern.)

## Does it matter how the policy was originally paid for?

Yes — this is the real starting point of the analysis. Three scenarios:

1. **Premiums remitted under the individual FX allowance**: a compliant channel; no retroactive clause applies, so no supplementary filing for existing policies. New policies after the individual rules take effect may need filing.
2. **Paid directly from offshore income**: the money never "left" China, so outbound-investment filing is not engaged at all.
3. **Large sums moved offshore via corporate accounts or informal channels**: the issue is not the policy but whether the original outbound path of the funds was compliant — case-by-case professional review is advised.

In every scenario: as long as the premium source is lawful, the policy remains protected under Hong Kong law. CRS will exchange its cash-value information as required, but information exchange does not make the policy unlawful.

## FAQ

**Q1: The decree took effect on 1 July — do I need to rush to file my old Hong Kong policy?**
No. Decree 837 has no retroactive clause; existing policies require no supplementary procedure. What to watch is the filing requirement for *new* purchases once the individual rules are issued.

**Q2: Are outbound-investment filing and CRS reporting the same thing?**
No. Filing governs the gate where money **leaves** China; CRS governs the gate where assets are **already offshore** (financial institutions exchanging account information with tax authorities). Neither replaces or exempts the other. See: [Outbound filing vs CRS reporting](outbound-filing-vs-crs-reporting.md).

**Q3: Does the new decree make Hong Kong policies "illegal" or not worth buying?**
No. Regulatory signals do not change the fundamental logic of Hong Kong insurance; suitability depends on the product, your needs, and your budget. If the individual rules eventually require filing for new purchases, that is one more procedure — not a veto.
