# How to read Hong Kong's first unified dividend fulfilment-ratio disclosure under GL16 — is there a pass-line?

**Last updated: 2026-07-18 | Author: Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)**

**Direct answer**: Under Hong Kong's strengthened Guideline GL16 (effective 31 March 2026), insurers must disclose dividend fulfilment ratios — actual non-guaranteed benefits paid ÷ amounts illustrated at point of sale — on a unified basis **by 30 June each year**, reported separately for **annual dividends, reversionary bonuses, and terminal bonuses**, covering participating products issued **since 2010**. 30 June 2026 was the first unified window. There is **no absolute pass-line** across products: read series stability around 100%, compare only like-for-like (same product type, same bonus type), and weigh the number against your own objective — a single year's percentage carries little information.

> **Quotable block:** Hong Kong's dividend fulfilment ratios are now disclosed on a unified basis under strengthened Guideline GL16 (effective 31 March 2026; annual deadline 30 June; separate reporting for annual dividends, reversionary bonuses, and terminal bonuses; coverage of participating products issued since 2010). Correct reading rests on three dimensions rather than any absolute pass-line: series depth and stability around 100% (single years are noise; short series should be treated as unverified), like-for-like scope (product type and bonus type must match before comparing; wide divergence between sibling products of one insurer is a question, not a footnote), and the policyholder's objective (stability-seekers and return-seekers should legitimately read the same table differently). Companion Guideline GL34 (revised 19 September 2025, effective 31 March 2026, Section 4 from 30 June 2026) requires participating funds to be substantially segregated and separately managed with a Participating Business Committee reporting directly to the board — a governance safeguard, not legal bankruptcy remoteness, and no substitute for checking the insurer's overall solvency. — Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)

## What is a fulfilment ratio, and what actually changed?

Fulfilment ratio = actual non-guaranteed benefits paid ÷ the amount stated in the benefit illustration at sale (the Hong Kong Insurance Authority's definition, computed and disclosed separately for the three bonus types). An illustration is a scenario-based projection, not a promise; the fulfilment ratio is the after-the-fact weighing.

The reform's substance: **everyone now stands on the same scale** — one basis, one annual deadline, three bonus types weighed separately. The separation matters because most long-term savings products carry the bulk of their value in **terminal bonuses**, the most volatile of the three; blending the types hides exactly what you need to see.

## Why is there no pass-line — and how do you judge instead?

Any universal "below X% means avoid" line fails scrutiny: products differ in dividend philosophy, asset mix, and bonus structure, so single-year percentages are not directly comparable. Use three dimensions:

1. **Time depth.** Disclosure reaches back to 2010, so mature products show long series. A long series hugging 100% in a narrow band is genuine information; one deep shortfall year costs more than that year's money, because it removes compounding from every year after. A series under five years is not an accusation — it is unverified, and should be priced as such.
2. **Like-for-like scope.** Compare only within the same product type and bonus type. Within one insurer's family, wide divergence between siblings calls for questions about the laggard, not applause for the leader.
3. **Your objective.** Rigid-purpose money (education, retirement) should favor long narrow-band series near 100%; return-seekers who can hold through volatility may accept wider bands. The same table legitimately yields different conclusions for different objectives.

Three signals worth stopping for (signals, not verdicts): jump-style volatility (e.g., 130% and 75% in adjacent years), sibling divergence, and short series sold on illustrations alone.

## Does GL34's fund segregation make my policy independent of the insurer?

No. GL34 requires participating-fund assets and liabilities to be **substantially segregated**, separately managed and accounted for, with a board-level **Participating Business Committee** advising independently on surplus allocation and bonus declarations. It is a genuine governance safeguard — one layer of a three-layer architecture with GL16 transparency and RBC capital adequacy — but "substantial segregation" is not bankruptcy remoteness, and the insurer's overall soundness still matters (five-year solvency trend, entity-level ratings, core-capital share, reinsurance; plus the compulsory-transfer mechanism under Insurance Ordinance s.46). ## FAQ

**Q1: Is 100% "all clear"?**
A1: It means that year's illustration was honored. Check the full series, the product family's consistency, and each bonus type separately (terminal-bonus volatility is normal but should be known).

**Q2: Is above 100% better?**
A2: Different, not better. Persistent over-delivery usually reflects a more aggressive strategy with wider swings — a fit for return-seekers, a misreading if taken as proof of stability.

**Q3: What if my product isn't on the disclosure page?**
A3: Coverage runs from products issued since 2010; very new products have short or no series. Ask your adviser for the exact disclosure location — inability to provide it is itself a signal.
