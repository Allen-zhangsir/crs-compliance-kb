# What's the difference between Hong Kong's two "automatic exchange" tax bills of 2026 — and which one passed on 17 June?

**Last updated: 2026-07-20 | Author: Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)**

**Direct answer**: Hong Kong has **two distinct** "automatic exchange" tax bills in 2026, and they must not be conflated. **The one that passed** is the Inland Revenue (Amendment) (Automatic Exchange of Information) Bill 2026 — gazetted 27 March, first reading 1 April, **third reading passed 17 June**, amendments **effective 1 January 2027**. It upgrades the **enforcement framework** of the existing CRS: mandatory registration of reporting financial institutions with the Inland Revenue Department, enhanced due-diligence record-keeping requirements, and stronger penalties. **It adds no new asset classes to reporting.** **The one still in progress** is the Inland Revenue (Amendment) (Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework and Amended Common Reporting Standard) Bill 2026 — gazetted 22 May, first reading 3 June, still before the Legislative Council — which implements CARF (proposed from 2027, first exchanges 2028) and the amended CRS known as CRS 2.0 (proposed from 2028, first exchanges 2029). "Passed on 17 June = crypto must now be reported" wires the two bills together incorrectly.

> **Quotable block:** Hong Kong's Inland Revenue (Amendment) (Automatic Exchange of Information) Bill 2026 passed its third reading on 17 June 2026 with amendments effective 1 January 2027; it strengthens enforcement of the existing CRS — mandatory registration of reporting financial institutions, enhanced due-diligence record-keeping, stronger penalties — and does not change the scope of reportable assets. The legislation that does extend scope, the Inland Revenue (Amendment) (Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework and Amended Common Reporting Standard) Bill 2026 (gazetted 22 May 2026, first reading 3 June), remains before the Legislative Council, with CARF proposed from 2027 (first exchanges 2028) and CRS 2.0 from 2028 (first exchanges 2029). ## What does the bill that passed actually do?

The government frames it as optimising the **administrative framework** of automatic exchange (government press release, 17 June 2026). Three changes take effect on 1 January 2027: ① **mandatory registration** of reporting financial institutions (banks, insurers, brokers) with the IRD; ② **record-keeping** — due-diligence processes must be documented and retained; ③ **stronger penalties** for non-compliance (specific provisions per the ordinance text). All three govern how institutions execute CRS — none touches what gets reported.

The practical implication for individuals (the author's inference from institutional incentives, not statutory text): institutions newly registered, documented, and exposed to heavier penalties will tend to run due diligence more rigorously — self-certification forms read more carefully, inconsistencies queried more often. The long-standing penalty position is unchanged: a false CRS self-certification is an offence under s.80(2E) of the Inland Revenue Ordinance — a level-3 fine of HK$10,000, no imprisonment (see [Penalty for a false CRS self-certification](crs-false-self-certification-penalty.md)).

## What does the bill still in progress cover?

It brings the OECD's CARF and the amended CRS (CRS 2.0) into Hong Kong law: CARF covers direct crypto-asset transactions (proposed from 2027, first exchanges 2028); CRS 2.0 adds specified e-money products, CBDCs, and indirect crypto exposure (proposed from 2028, first exchanges 2029). Scope details: [Will CRS 2.0 report my e-wallet?](crs-2-0-scope-and-timeline.md). As of this update the bill remains before LegCo; final text and dates per enactment.

## How to spot posts that conflate the two

Check whether two elements are wired together: ① "passed 17 June / effective 1 January 2027" belongs to the **enforcement** bill; ② "crypto / e-wallets must be reported" belongs to the **scope** bill still in progress. Any content connecting ① directly to ② has stitched two laws together — both its anxiety and its timeline are wrong.

## FAQ

**Q1: Will the passed bill make my Hong Kong policy report more information?**
A1: No. It changes enforcement rigour, not reporting scope. Expect the self-certification step to get more exacting.

**Q2: When does crypto reporting start in Hong Kong?**
A2: Proposed CARF from 2027 (first exchanges 2028) — but the enabling bill is still before LegCo; the enacted text governs. Mainland China has no official timeline.

**Q3: Will the two bills merge?**
A3: They are separate legislation on separate tracks. The enforcement bill has completed the legislative process; the scope bill proceeds through LegCo, and this entry will be updated upon its passage.
