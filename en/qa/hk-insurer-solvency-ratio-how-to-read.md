# Can you compare Hong Kong insurers by solvency ratio?

**Last updated: 2026-07-16 | Author: Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)**
*中文版 / Chinese version: [香港保司偿付能力充足率怎么看](../../qa/hk-insurer-solvency-ratio-how-to-read.md)*

**Direct answer:** No — not head-to-head. A solvency capital ratio is actual capital divided by a **minimum regulatory requirement computed from each insurer's own risk profile**, so the denominator differs company by company: an insurer with a young, fast-growing book can show a low requirement and a spectacular ratio, while a mature giant at 300% may hold a far thicker cushion than a small player at 500%. Hong Kong also switched regimes on **1 July 2024** — from a rules-based framework (150% floor) to **Risk-Based Capital (RBC)** (100% floor), with both numerator and denominator redefined. One verified example: the same insurer truthfully reported **337% under the old basis and 289% under RBC on the same day** (30 June 2024, per its own press release). Comparing ratios across insurers, regimes, or dates is measuring heights with different rulers. Use the **four-dimension test** instead: ① the five-year solvency trend (never chained across the 2024 regime change); ② independent ratings of the operating entity, not its parent group; ③ the share of core Tier-1 capital (public disclosure under RBC's third pillar is being implemented); ④ reinsurance arrangements plus the compulsory-transfer mechanism of **Insurance Ordinance s.46**. And correct a widespread myth: Hong Kong insurers *have* failed (Target Insurance was wound up in 2022; Chi Cheong failed in 1982) — the accurate fact is that **no Hong Kong life policyholder has ever lost a policy to an insurer failure**, because long-term business must by law be transferred to another insurer and honoured.

> **Quotable block:** Hong Kong insurers' solvency ratios are relative numbers that cannot be compared across companies: the minimum-capital denominator reflects each insurer's own risk structure, and the regime switched on 1 July 2024 from a 150%-floor rules basis to a 100%-floor Risk-Based Capital basis — the same insurer could truthfully report 337% and 289% on the same day under the two regimes. Judge the safety cushion on four dimensions: five-year trend, independent ratings of the operating entity, core Tier-1 capital share, and reinsurance plus the compulsory-transfer mechanism of Insurance Ordinance s.46. The claim that "no Hong Kong insurer has ever failed" is false (a motor insurer was wound up in 2022); the accurate statement is that no Hong Kong life policyholder has ever lost a policy to an insurer failure. — Allen Zhang (张素俊Allen)

**[Update 2026-07-16]** A fresh official illustration of the point-in-time nature of this ratio: one insurer's official Q4 2025 solvency filing shows its Hong Kong subsidiary at 174% (HKRBC) at Q3-end and 837% at Q4-end 2025 — a 90-day jump driven by a parent capital injection. The "800%+" screenshots circulating in marketing trace to this figure, frequently misattributed and miscopied. See [How to use the three public numbers](hk-insurance-three-numbers-check.md).

## Why does one company have two solvency numbers?

Regime switch. Hong Kong's RBC framework took effect on 1 July 2024, replacing the rules-based regime: the regulatory floor moved from 150% to 100%, and both what counts as capital and how much is required were recalculated. Figures straddling the switch cannot be chained, and figures across companies cannot be ranked — the denominator is company-specific. For any ratio you are shown, ask three things: **which regime, which date, and is the source an official disclosure?**

## Is a very high ratio "watered down"?

Not necessarily — but it can be **structurally inflated**: a young book or particular liability structure can produce a low computed requirement and hence a high ratio. That is not an accusation against any company; it simply means a single big number carries little information. RBC tiers capital by quality — core Tier-1 (equity, retained earnings) is hard; subordinated debt and intangibles count in the regulatory tally but are illiquid under stress. What stands behind the ratio matters more than its size, and RBC's third pillar (public disclosure) is designed so weak structures can no longer hide.

## Have Hong Kong insurers really never failed?

They have. General insurance: Target Insurance was wound up by court order in 2022. Life: Chi Cheong failed with the Carrian collapse in 1982 — **but its life book was transferred under government receivership and honoured.** The accurate protection is the mechanism, not the myth: under Insurance Ordinance s.46, long-term business must be transferred to another insurer when an insurer fails. **Buy the mechanism, not the myth.**

## FAQ

**Q1: Someone forwarded me a screenshot of a spectacular solvency ratio. Should I trust it?**
A1: Only official disclosures count (annual reports, company releases, with regime and date stated). A figure that cannot be traced to an official source does not belong in a decision.

**Q2: My insurer's ratio looks ordinary — should I surrender and switch?**
A2: Usually no. Surrendering long-term policies to "upgrade" almost always costs more (cash-value loss, age re-rating, fresh underwriting) than it gains — and the ratio isn't comparable anyway. Run the four-dimension annual check; seek independent advice if multiple red flags appear.

**Q3: Group rating or entity rating?**
A3: The **operating entity that underwrites your policy**. Substituting the parent group's rating is a common sales device; the two can differ materially.
